	## AMD EPYC – Core Count & Efficiency

- **Past 5 years**:
    
    - Max core count increased from **64 → 192 cores**.
        
    - Maintained/improved **per-core performance**.
        
    - **Same socket** and **physical footprint**.
- **Low-core CPUs**:
    
    - Increased **clock speeds**.
        
    - Improved **power efficiency**.

## NVIDIA Datacenter GPUs – Locked Features

- **3 new architectures** released in 2 years.
    
- Major architectural & feature advancements.
    
- Significant **firmware improvements** too — **never reach consumers**.

### NVLink

- Originally on consumer GPUs → **removed**.
    
- **Enterprise-only** now.
    
- Would benefit modern games (e.g. shared VRAM).


### Hardware Decompression

- Enterprise-only decompression engines.
    
- Capable of **900 GB/s** on-the-fly decompression.
    
- Could massively reduce game sizes if made available.

## Industry Strategy & Profit Motive

- NVIDIA under-allocates VRAM on consumer cards.
    
- Locks powerful features behind enterprise pricing.
    
- Gamers = ~**20% of revenue**, AI/data center = priority.
    
- Narrative of "hardware limits" is marketing → real limit is **architectural**.


## Real-World Pricing Example

- **24/48-core EPYC CPU** bought for same price as current-gen Ryzen 5.
    
- No scheduler issues (unlike Intel 12th gen+).
    
    - Enterprise CPUs use **hardware schedulers** that actually work.

## Process Node Comparison

- Consumer hardware = **5–7nm**.
    
- Enterprise hardware = **4nm**.

## Apple – Outlier in Consumer Innovation

- Apple relies purely on **consumer sales** → actual innovation.
    
- Makes **fastest, most efficient** consumer CPU + GPU.
    
- Enterprise = same chips, no price hike.
    
- **Mac Mini ≈ $800 AUD** = full system for the price of a GPU like the 5070.
